Complete Mango Cultivation Guide: From Planting to Harvest

Everything a farmer needs to know to grow healthy, high-yielding mango trees in tropical and subtropical climates.

📖 12 min read 🌾 GreenHarvest Editorial 📅 2025

Mango (Mangifera indica) is one of the most widely cultivated and commercially important fruit crops in the world. Known as the "King of Fruits," mango trees are grown across tropical and subtropical regions and offer farmers an excellent long-term income when managed well. This guide covers everything from site selection to harvest.

1. Climate & Soil Requirements

Mango trees thrive in tropical and subtropical climates. They require a distinct dry season to flower and fruit well. The ideal temperature range is between 24°C and 30°C, though established trees can tolerate temperatures up to 48°C and brief cold spells down to 5°C.

🌡️ Key Climate Facts Mango trees need a dry period of at least 2–3 months before flowering season. Areas with high humidity during flowering experience fruit drop and fungal disease issues.

Well-drained, deep loamy or alluvial soil with a pH between 5.5 and 7.5 is ideal. Mango trees are moderately tolerant of poor soils but perform best in fertile, organic-rich ground. Avoid waterlogged areas, as root rot is one of the most common causes of mango tree failure.

2. Selecting the Right Variety

Choosing the right mango variety is one of the most important decisions a farmer will make. Different varieties suit different markets, climates, and farming objectives.

3. Propagation Methods

Mango trees can be propagated through seeds or vegetative methods. For commercial cultivation, vegetative propagation is always preferred because it produces true-to-type trees that fruit much earlier than seedlings.

Grafting (Most Recommended)

Veneer grafting and epicotyl grafting are the most widely used methods. Grafted trees begin fruiting in just 3–4 years compared to 6–8 years for seedling trees. Grafts also ensure uniform fruit quality across your entire orchard.

Budding

Shield budding is a fast and efficient technique suited for large-scale nursery production. Success rates of 80–90% are achievable with proper technique and timing.

4. Planting

The best time to plant mango is at the beginning of the monsoon season or in early spring when irrigation is available. This allows roots to establish firmly before the first dry season stress.

  • Spacing: 8–10 metres for standard varieties; 5–6 metres for high-density planting
  • Pit size: 1m × 1m × 1m — fill with topsoil mixed with 20 kg compost
  • Planting depth: Same as the nursery depth — do not bury the graft union
  • Staking: Stake young trees for the first 2 years to prevent wind damage

5. Irrigation

Young mango trees require regular watering until established. Mature trees are relatively drought-tolerant but irrigation during critical periods dramatically increases yield and fruit quality.

💧 Critical Irrigation Periods Water stress 1–2 months before flowering actually encourages better flower induction. However, adequate moisture during fruit development (marble stage to harvest) is essential to prevent fruit drop and cracking.

Drip irrigation is highly recommended for commercial orchards. It reduces water use by 30–40%, prevents leaf wetness that encourages fungal disease, and can be combined with fertigation to deliver nutrients directly to the root zone.

6. Fertilization Schedule

Apply fertilizers in two split doses — once after harvest and once before flowering. Always water after fertilizer application to prevent root burn.

7. Pest & Disease Management

Mango trees are susceptible to several pests and diseases. Early identification and prompt action are the keys to minimising losses.

Common Pests

  • Mango Hoppers: The most serious pest. Spray imidacloprid or neem oil at 5% concentration at bud burst stage.
  • Fruit Flies: Use protein bait traps and pheromone traps. Bag fruit at marble stage for premium quality.
  • Mealybugs: Apply neem oil spray and use sticky banding on tree trunks to prevent crawling up.
  • Stem Borers: Paint the trunk with a lime + copper sulphate mixture annually.

Common Diseases

  • Powdery Mildew: Most damaging during flowering. Spray wettable sulfur or carbendazim at first sign.
  • Anthracnose: Causes black spots on fruit. Use copper-based fungicide spray every 10–14 days during monsoon.
  • Bacterial Canker: Prune affected branches and apply Bordeaux paste on cut surfaces.

8. Harvesting

Mango trees typically begin bearing fruit in their 3rd–4th year after grafting and reach full production by year 8–10. A well-maintained mature tree yields 100–200 kg of fruit per season.

Harvest indicators vary by variety, but generally include a change in skin colour from deep green to lighter green or yellow, a slight softening of the fruit shoulders, and the development of characteristic aroma. Harvest in the cool morning hours to extend shelf life.

✂️
Prune After Harvest

Remove crossing branches and reduce canopy height each year for better light penetration and easier management.

🌿
Mulch the Base

Apply 10 cm of organic mulch around the tree base to retain moisture and improve soil health.

📋
Keep Records

Track fertilizer applications, spray schedules and yields to improve management year on year.

💰
Intercrop Early

Grow short-duration vegetables or legumes between young trees for income during the first 4–5 years.

Conclusion

Mango cultivation is a long-term investment that rewards patient, attentive farmers with decades of reliable income. By selecting the right variety, preparing your site well, and following a consistent management schedule, you can build a productive mango orchard that becomes a lasting asset for your family and community.

Banana Farming: High Yield Techniques for Tropical Climates

Proven strategies to increase your banana yield by up to 40% using modern cultivation methods.

📖 8 min read🌾 GreenHarvest

Banana is one of the most important fruit crops worldwide. It is fast-growing, productive, and can generate income within 9–12 months of planting. This guide covers high-yield techniques that modern banana farmers are using to dramatically improve their harvests.

1. Variety Selection

Choosing a disease-resistant, high-yielding variety is the foundation of successful banana farming. Cavendish varieties (Grand Naine, Robusta) are the most widely grown for commercial markets. For organic and local markets, consider Red Banana or Nendran varieties that command a premium price.

2. Soil Preparation & Planting

Bananas need deep, well-drained, fertile soil with a pH of 6.0–7.0. Prepare the field with deep tillage to break up hardpan layers. Dig planting pits of 60 × 60 × 60 cm and fill with a mix of topsoil, compost, and rock phosphate. Space plants 1.8 × 1.8 metres for high-density planting.

3. Tissue Culture Plants

Using tissue culture (TC) plants instead of suckers is one of the biggest yield improvements available to banana farmers. TC plants are virus-free, uniform, and produce 20–30% higher yields than sucker-grown plants. They also fruit 4–6 weeks earlier.

4. Irrigation & Nutrition

Banana is a water-intensive crop requiring 25–30 mm of water per week. Drip irrigation with fertigation delivers the best results. Apply nitrogen in split doses every 3 weeks during the vegetative phase, and increase potassium during bunch development for better fruit size and quality.

5. Bunch Management

Remove male flowers after the last hand has formed to direct energy to fruit development. Cover bunches with perforated blue polyethylene bags at 3–4 weeks after emergence. This protects against pest damage, improves colour, and can increase bunch weight by 15–20%.

💡 Pro Tip Leave only the best ratoon sucker per plant after the first harvest. A well-managed ratoon crop gives nearly the same yield as the plant crop with significantly lower input costs.

How Drone Technology is Revolutionizing Modern Farming

From crop monitoring to precision spraying, agricultural drones are changing the way farmers work — and cutting costs dramatically.

📖 6 min read🌾 GreenHarvest Tech

Agricultural drones have gone from a novelty to an essential farming tool in just a few years. Today, farmers across the world are using drones to monitor crop health, spray pesticides, assess irrigation needs, and even plant seeds — all at a fraction of the traditional cost and time.

What Can Drones Do on a Farm?

  • Crop Health Monitoring: NDVI sensors detect plant stress weeks before it becomes visible to the naked eye, allowing early intervention.
  • Precision Spraying: Drone sprayers apply pesticides and fertilizers with 90% less water use and 30–40% less chemical use compared to conventional methods.
  • Field Mapping: High-resolution aerial maps help farmers identify problem areas, measure field boundaries, and plan irrigation systems.
  • Seeding: Multicopter drones can seed cover crops and direct-seeded crops at a rate of 5–10 acres per hour.

Cost vs Benefit

A basic agricultural monitoring drone costs between ₹50,000 and ₹2,00,000. Spraying drones range from ₹2,00,000 to ₹8,00,000. For medium to large farms, the ROI is typically achieved within 1–2 seasons through reduced labour costs, lower chemical use, and higher yields from better crop monitoring.

Getting Started

In India, drone operators require a Remote Pilot Certificate (RPC) from DGCA. Many state agriculture departments offer subsidies of 40–50% on agricultural drone purchases. Drone-as-a-service providers are also available in most farming regions, allowing farmers to access drone services without purchasing equipment.

🚁 Did You Know? Studies show that drone-based crop monitoring can increase overall farm productivity by 15–25% by enabling timely, targeted interventions rather than blanket treatments.

Coconut Cultivation: Complete Care Guide

From seedling selection to first harvest — everything you need to grow healthy, productive coconut palms.

📖 10 min read🌾 GreenHarvest

Coconut (Cocos nucifera) is one of the most valuable and versatile tropical crops. Every part of the coconut tree has economic value — from the fruit to the husk, shell, leaves, and trunk. A well-managed coconut palm can produce for 60–80 years, making it one of agriculture's best long-term investments.

Climate & Soil

Coconut thrives in humid tropical coastal areas with temperatures between 27°C and 32°C and annual rainfall of 1500–2500 mm. Sandy loam or laterite soils with good drainage are ideal. The tree tolerates saline soils better than most fruit crops.

Variety Selection

Tall varieties (West Coast Tall, East Coast Tall) are hardy and long-lived but take 6–8 years to first bear fruit. Dwarf varieties (Chowghat Orange Dwarf, Malayan Yellow Dwarf) fruit in 3–4 years and are better suited to small farms. Hybrid varieties (T × D, D × T) combine the best of both — early bearing and high yield.

Planting

Select mature, fully ripe seedling nuts that are 10–12 months old. Plant in pits of 1 × 1 × 1 metre at a spacing of 7.5 × 7.5 metres (triangular system) for best results. Plant at the start of the southwest monsoon. Apply 25 kg compost per pit before planting.

💡 Key InsightRegular application of salt (common table salt or sodium chloride) at 2 kg per palm per year has been shown to dramatically increase coconut yield in trials across South India.

Fertilization

Apply NPK fertilizers twice per year — before the onset of monsoon and at the close of monsoon. A mature bearing palm requires 500g N, 320g P₂O₅, and 1200g K₂O per year, along with 50 kg of compost or organic manure. Coconut is highly responsive to potassium fertilization.

Papaya Growing: Fast Fruiting Strategies

Papaya can fruit in just 9 months. Learn how to maximise speed and yield with these expert strategies.

📖 7 min read🌾 GreenHarvest

Papaya (Carica papaya) is one of the fastest-fruiting tropical crops available to farmers. Under good management, plants can begin producing fruit in as little as 9–11 months from planting. This makes papaya an excellent choice for generating quick cash flow while longer-term crops establish.

Best Varieties for High Yield

Red Lady F1, Taiwan 786, and Pusa Delicious are among the highest-yielding papaya varieties available. These hybrids are gynoecious (all female) or hermaphrodite and produce consistently high-quality fruits weighing 1–2 kg each. Avoid dioecious varieties for commercial production unless you can manage the 1:10 male-to-female planting ratio carefully.

Rapid Establishment Tips

Start seeds in polybags filled with a mixture of cocopeat, compost, and vermiculite. Transplant at 25–30 days when seedlings are 15 cm tall. Never allow papaya roots to become waterlogged — even brief flooding can be fatal to young plants. Apply starter fertilizer (high phosphorus) at transplanting to encourage fast root development.

Virus Management

Papaya Ring Spot Virus (PRSV) is the biggest threat to papaya production worldwide. Use virus-free seedlings, control aphid vectors with reflective mulch and neem oil sprays, and remove infected plants immediately before the virus spreads.

Jackfruit Cultivation: The Giant Tropical Fruit

Jackfruit is one of the world's largest tree fruits. Here is how to grow it right from day one.

📖 9 min read🌾 GreenHarvest

Jackfruit (Artocarpus heterophyllus) is the world's largest tree-borne fruit, capable of weighing up to 50 kg. It is a remarkably low-input crop that tolerates poor soils and drought once established. With growing global demand for jackfruit as a meat substitute, its commercial potential has never been greater.

Climate & Soil Requirements

Jackfruit is adapted to humid tropical and near-tropical conditions. It grows best at temperatures between 16°C and 35°C with annual rainfall of 1000–2400 mm. Unlike many tropical fruits, jackfruit is moderately tolerant of drought and grows in a wide range of soils including laterite, red loam, and alluvial soils with good drainage.

Propagation

Jackfruit is traditionally grown from seed, but seeds are recalcitrant — they must be planted within 30 days of extraction from the fruit. For commercial cultivation, use budded or grafted plants from a reputable nursery. Grafted trees bear fruit in 3–4 years versus 5–7 years for seedlings, and produce more uniform, higher-quality fruit.

Spacing & Intercropping

Plant jackfruit at 8 × 8 metres spacing. In the early years, the wide spacing provides an excellent opportunity to intercrop with banana, pineapple, turmeric, or ginger. These short-term crops generate income while jackfruit trees establish, and the organic matter from their residues improves soil health for the trees.

🌍 Market OpportunityGlobal jackfruit exports have grown over 300% in the last decade, driven by demand in the USA, UK, and EU as a vegan meat alternative. Premium grades can fetch ₹40–₹80 per kg at the farm gate.
