Growing Mango Organically: What the Textbooks Don't Tell You

A candid, experience-driven guide to cultivating mango trees without synthetic chemicals — and getting better results because of it.

📖 14 min read🌿 GreenHarvestOrganic Methods

Let's be honest — most mango cultivation guides read like they were written in a government office. Pages of tables and generic advice that rarely accounts for the fact that every orchard is different, every soil has its own character, and every farmer has constraints that a textbook writer never considered.

This guide is different. It is written for the farmer who wants to grow mangoes without depending on a chemical shop, who wants fruit that actually tastes like mango, and who wants a tree that will still be productive in thirty years without poisoning the ground beneath it.

1. Understanding What a Mango Tree Actually Needs

Before we talk about planting or pruning, it helps to understand the mango's natural logic. Mango trees evolved in tropical Asia — they are built for heat, seasonal dry spells, and occasional flooding. Their greatest need is not fertiliser or pesticide. It is timing. Get the timing right, and a mango tree almost grows itself.

The single most important thing to understand is this: mango trees flower in response to water stress followed by cool, dry weather. If your tree is sitting in constantly moist soil year-round, it will grow beautifully and never produce fruit. A period of deliberate water restriction before the flowering season is not neglect — it is management.

🔑 The Core Principle

Mango trees need stress to flower. A dry period of 6–8 weeks before your region's traditional flowering season tells the tree it is time to reproduce. This is the most important thing an organic mango farmer can control.

2. Choosing Your Variety

This is a decision that will define your orchard for the next fifty years, so it deserves more thought than most farmers give it. Rather than chasing the most expensive or fashionable variety, choose based on three factors: your climate, your intended market, and disease resistance.

For export or premium fresh markets, Alphonso and Kesar are globally recognized and command strong prices. For processing into pulp, Totapuri's reliability and high fibre content makes it ideal. If you are in a region with high humidity and fungal disease pressure, look for local varieties that have adapted to those conditions over generations — they will outlast any imported variety.

3. Organic Soil Preparation

In conventional farming, soil is treated as a medium that holds roots in place while you pour fertiliser on them. In organic farming, soil is the product. Get your soil right and the tree feeds itself. The difference is profound — both in cost savings and in long-term productivity.

Before you plant a single tree, invest in a soil test. Know your pH, your organic matter content, and your major and minor nutrient levels. A soil pH between 5.5 and 7.5 suits mangoes, but if you are at the extremes of this range, lime or sulphur applications a full season before planting will save you years of problems.

Building the Planting Pit

Dig a pit of at least 1 metre cubed. This is larger than most guides suggest, and for good reason. The disturbed, loose soil in a large pit encourages early root expansion, which directly translates to faster establishment and earlier fruiting. Fill the pit with a mixture of topsoil, mature compost, and well-rotted farm manure in roughly equal parts.

💡 The Trichoderma Secret

Before filling your pit, mix Trichoderma viride or T. harzianum powder into your compost at about 10g per kg of compost. This beneficial fungus colonises the root zone, suppresses soil-borne pathogens like Phytophthora (which causes root rot), and actively improves nutrient availability. It is inexpensive, universally available, and one of the most overlooked tools in organic fruit growing.

4. Planting and Establishment

Plant at the beginning of your rainy season so the tree receives natural rainfall during its most vulnerable period. Space trees a minimum of 8 metres apart — the temptation to plant closer for a higher initial yield is understandable, but crowded trees suffer from poor airflow, which drives fungal disease pressure up significantly.

If you are using grafted plants, which we strongly recommend, ensure the graft union sits at least 10 centimetres above the soil line. Burying the graft union is one of the most common planting mistakes, and it creates an entry point for soil-borne diseases that can kill trees years later.

5. Organic Nutrition Through the Tree's Life

The organic approach to mango nutrition is not about replacing each chemical fertiliser with an organic equivalent. It is about building a living soil that cycles nutrients continuously, reducing your need to intervene at all. The goal is a self-sustaining system, not a permanent dependency on inputs of any kind.

Compost and Mulch as the Foundation

Apply 20–30 kg of well-composted organic matter per tree twice a year — once before the rains begin and once after harvest. Combine this with a thick mulch layer of dry leaves, straw, or wood chips around the base of the tree (keeping it away from the trunk). This mulch does several things simultaneously: it retains moisture, regulates soil temperature, suppresses weeds, and slowly decomposes to feed the soil biology.

Bio-Fertilisers Worth Using

Pseudomonas fluorescens as a soil drench improves phosphorus availability and also acts as a biological control against several bacterial diseases. Applied at planting and twice yearly thereafter, it has shown consistent results across diverse growing conditions worldwide. Azospirillum and Azotobacter inoculants fix atmospheric nitrogen in the root zone, partially replacing the need for nitrogen-rich organic inputs.

6. Managing Pests and Disease Without Chemicals

The single most effective pest management strategy in organic mango growing is diversity. An orchard that contains only mango trees is an invitation to pest outbreaks. Planting nitrogen-fixing trees between rows, maintaining ground cover, and allowing beneficial insects to establish themselves creates a natural balance that chemical spraying permanently disrupts.

Mango Hopper — The Real Threat

Mango hopper is the most economically damaging mango pest globally. Neem oil at 3–5ml per litre of water, sprayed at bud burst and again just before flowering, suppresses hopper populations effectively without harming pollinators. Neem also has a residual effect that deters the next generation from establishing on the same tree.

Powdery Mildew on Flowers

Powdery mildew can devastate a flowering crop. The organic control is a spray of diluted buttermilk (1 part to 9 parts water) applied in the evening. This sounds unusual, but the lactic acid bacteria in buttermilk outcompete the mildew fungus on leaf and flower surfaces. It works, it costs almost nothing, and it has been validated across multiple research trials in India and beyond.

7. The Harvest and What Comes After

A well-managed organic mango tree should begin bearing meaningfully in its fourth or fifth year and reach full production by year eight. At that point, expect 80–150 kg per tree annually depending on variety and management. The key post-harvest task is pruning — removing crossing branches, reducing canopy height, and thinning out crowded growth. This single practice, done consistently each year after harvest, is more responsible for long-term yield than almost anything else a farmer can do.

🌿
Mulch Always

A permanent mulch layer is the single most impactful thing you can do for an established mango tree. It builds the soil while you sleep.

✂️
Prune Every Year

Annual post-harvest pruning keeps the tree productive for decades. Skip it for two years in a row and you will spend five years recovering.

💧
Stress Before Flowering

Withhold irrigation for 6–8 weeks before your expected flowering period. It feels counterintuitive but it is essential.

🦠
Use Trichoderma

Add Trichoderma to every planting pit and to your compost. It is the most cost-effective soil health investment available to organic farmers.

Banana Farming Without Chemicals: A Practical Guide

Bananas are one of the most chemically intensive commercial crops in the world. Here is how to grow them differently — and better.

📖 9 min read🌿 GreenHarvest

Commercial banana production has a troubled relationship with chemistry. Monoculture plantations, heavy fungicide applications, and soil exhaustion have made banana farming increasingly expensive and environmentally damaging. But the banana plant itself is remarkably resilient — it is the farming system around it that has become fragile.

This guide is for the farmer growing bananas at any scale who wants to reduce or eliminate chemical inputs without sacrificing yield. The methods described here have been practised successfully across Asia, Africa, and Latin America in widely varying conditions.

Why Organic Banana Farming Works

Banana plants are naturally heavy feeders and fast growers. The conventional response to this is constant chemical fertilisation. The organic response is to work with the banana's biology — using its own spent plant material as the primary input, cycling nutrients back into the soil continuously.

After each harvest, the pseudostem (the main stalk) is chopped and left to decompose in place. A single pseudostem contains significant quantities of potassium, calcium, and micronutrients. Chopping and mulching this material returns it to the soil within four to six weeks. On a well-managed organic banana farm, this practice alone can replace 40–60% of the potassium inputs that would otherwise need to be applied.

🍌 The Pseudostem Cycle

Never remove and discard a spent banana pseudostem. Chop it into 30cm sections, lay it flat between rows as mulch, and let it decompose in place. It is free fertiliser, moisture retention, and weed suppression all at once.

Soil and Planting

Bananas need deep, well-drained soil with high organic matter content and a pH between 6.0 and 7.5. Before planting, incorporate at least 10kg of mature compost per planting hole and inoculate with Mycorrhizal fungi — these create a symbiotic relationship with banana roots that dramatically improves water and phosphorus uptake.

Tissue culture plants are strongly recommended for organic production because they start disease-free, which gives the organic management system the best possible foundation. The premium paid for TC plants over suckers is recovered within the first harvest cycle through more uniform, higher-quality fruit.

Organic Pest and Disease Management

Fusarium wilt (Panama disease) is the most serious threat to banana production globally. There is no organic cure once a plant is infected. Prevention is everything — use resistant varieties where available, avoid moving soil from infected areas, and maintain high soil biological activity through compost applications and bio-inoculants, as healthy soil suppresses Fusarium naturally.

For nematode management, which is the other major challenge in banana, marigold interplanting has shown significant suppressive effects. Plant African marigold densely between rows, chop them in at the end of their growth cycle, and you will reduce nematode populations with no chemical input at all.

Avocado Cultivation: The Organic Way to Grow the World's Favourite Fruit

Avocado demand has never been higher. Here is how to meet it without chemicals — and why organic avocados command a significant price premium.

📖 10 min read🌿 GreenHarvest

The avocado has gone from a regional food to one of the world's most sought-after fruits in less than a generation. Demand continues to rise in Europe, North America, and across Asia. For a farmer willing to invest the time to establish an orchard and manage it organically, avocado represents one of the most financially rewarding long-term crops available.

Organic avocados sell for 30–60% more than conventionally grown fruit in most international markets. The certification process takes time, but the premium is persistent and growing.

Climate and Site Selection

Avocado is more climate-sensitive than most tropical fruit trees. It needs mild temperatures — ideally 15°C to 28°C — and is highly intolerant of frost and waterlogging. The roots are extremely sensitive to oxygen deprivation, which is why drainage is the single most critical site selection criterion. A site where water pools even briefly after heavy rain is unsuitable without major drainage work.

Altitudes between 1,000 and 2,500 metres in tropical regions often provide ideal conditions — cool nights that improve fruit quality, good drainage on slopes, and reduced disease pressure compared to humid lowlands. Kenya, Colombia, and parts of southern India have emerged as major organic avocado producers precisely because these elevation conditions exist there.

Varieties for Organic Production

Hass is the dominant commercial variety globally, accounting for around 80% of world trade. It is well-suited to organic production because of its thick, pebbly skin which gives it good post-harvest handling resilience. For humid tropical climates where Hass struggles with fungal disease, local varieties adapted to those conditions are a better starting point even if they command lower prices initially.

Organic Soil Management

Avocado roots are shallow and sensitive. They occupy the top 60cm of soil and spread far beyond the canopy. This means that what you do to the soil surface across your entire orchard affects your trees — not just what you apply at the base. A permanent ground cover of leguminous plants under and between trees adds nitrogen continuously, prevents erosion, and builds organic matter year after year.

🌿 Avocado's Greatest Enemy

Phytophthora root rot is responsible for more avocado tree deaths than all other causes combined. Trichoderma harzianum applied as a soil drench twice yearly is the most effective organic preventive measure available. Combined with excellent drainage and avoiding any soil compaction near the root zone, it can prevent the disease in most situations.

Pollination and Fruit Set

Avocado flowers have an unusual behaviour — the same flower opens as female in the morning and male in the afternoon on alternating days. This means that planting two complementary varieties (Type A and Type B) close together is essential for good fruit set. Without cross-pollination, fruit set can be as low as 1% even in a healthy, flowering tree. With good cross-pollination and a healthy bee population, fruit set rises to 3–8% — which is all a productive avocado tree needs.

Moringa: The Tree That Feeds, Heals, and Enriches Your Soil

No tree on earth offers more per square metre of land than Moringa. This is everything you need to know to grow it well.

📖 8 min read🌿 GreenHarvest

If you had to choose one tree to plant in a food-insecure region with degraded soil and unreliable rainfall, most agronomists who know it would choose Moringa. It grows in near-desert conditions. Its leaves contain more protein than most legumes. Its roots and seed cake improve soil structure. And it can be harvested multiple times per year.

Moringa oleifera is sometimes called the "miracle tree" — and while that label is often over-applied in the wellness industry, the plant's actual agricultural and nutritional credentials are genuinely remarkable.

Why Moringa Suits Organic Farming Perfectly

Moringa requires almost no inputs to grow well. It fixes no nitrogen itself, but its deep taproot mines nutrients from subsoil layers that shallow-rooted crops cannot access, then deposits them on the surface through leaf fall. When used as a component of an agroforestry system — planted in rows between vegetable beds or annual crops — it functions as a living fertiliser factory.

Pest pressure on Moringa is genuinely low. The leaves contain isothiocyanates and other compounds that most insects find unpalatable. In over a decade of observing Moringa cultivation across different continents, serious pest damage is rare. This makes it one of the easiest crops to manage organically.

Cultivation Basics

Moringa can be grown from seed or from cuttings. Cuttings of 1–2 metres in length taken from mature stems root readily and produce a fruiting tree faster than seedlings. Plant at any spacing depending on your objective — 2.5 metres for leaf production, 5 metres for drumstick (pod) production, or 10+ metres as an agroforestry component among other crops.

The tree grows astonishingly fast — up to 3 metres in its first year in good conditions. For leaf production, cut back to 1 metre every two to three months to maintain bushy, manageable growth and maximise leaf yield per plant. For pod production, allow the tree to develop a full canopy structure and prune only to remove dead wood and crossing branches.

🌿 Moringa as a Bio-Fertiliser

A fermented extract of fresh Moringa leaves diluted 1:30 with water and used as a foliar spray has been shown in multiple studies to increase crop yields by 20–35% when applied to vegetables and cereals. The mechanism is not fully understood, but the practical results are consistent. It is free, it is natural, and it works.

Nutritional and Commercial Value

Fresh Moringa leaves contain significant quantities of Vitamin C, Vitamin A, calcium, potassium, and all essential amino acids. They can be eaten fresh, cooked as a vegetable, or dried and powdered for a supplement that has found a substantial global market. The dried leaf powder commands strong prices in health food markets across Europe, North America, and Japan.

Moringa seed oil — pressed from the seeds — is used in cosmetics and cooking. The seed cake remaining after oil extraction is an excellent organic soil amendment with strong water-clarifying properties. Every part of this tree has value. That is genuinely rare in agriculture.

Coconut Cultivation: Organic Methods That Actually Work

A coconut palm planted today will feed your grandchildren. Here is how to give it the best possible start — without a single synthetic input.

📖 10 min read🌿 GreenHarvest

The coconut palm is one of humanity's oldest and most important cultivated plants. Across tropical coastlines worldwide, it has provided food, fibre, oil, timber, and income for generations. A well-sited and well-managed coconut palm will produce for 60 to 80 years — making the decisions made at planting among the most consequential a farmer ever takes.

Selecting Your Seedling

The quality of your seedling determines the productivity of your orchard for the next half century. Select nuts from high-yielding mother palms that are 10 to 12 months old, fully mature, and have a round or oval shape — elongated nuts tend to produce less productive palms. Germinate nuts in a shaded nursery bed in sand, checking after 3 months for sprouts. Transplant only seedlings that are vigorous and symmetrical.

Organic Nutrition for Coconut

Coconut is one of the most responsive crops to organic matter application. Annual applications of 30–50kg of compost combined with 2kg of wood ash (for potassium, which coconuts need in large quantities) and vermicompost have produced yield improvements of 25–40% in farms that previously received no organic inputs.

One of the most interesting and well-documented inputs for coconut is common salt — sodium chloride. Research across Kerala, Sri Lanka, and the Philippines has consistently shown that 1–2kg of salt applied to the root zone of each palm twice yearly increases nut set and improves copra quality. The mechanism involves sodium's role in replacing potassium in certain metabolic functions, essentially stretching the palm's potassium supply further. For inland palms especially, this is worth trying.

💡 Intercropping Under Coconut

The space beneath coconut palms — which receives 50–70% of full sunlight — is some of the most underused agricultural land in the tropics. Turmeric, ginger, pineapple, and cacao all thrive in this environment. A well-managed coconut-based agroforestry system can generate 3–5 times more income per hectare than coconuts alone.

Papaya: How to Get Your First Harvest in 9 Months, Organically

No other fruit gives you a return on investment this quickly. Here is how to make the most of it without chemicals.

📖 7 min read🌿 GreenHarvest

Papaya is the fastest path from planting to income in tropical fruit production. Under good conditions with organic management, a papaya plant can be fruiting within 9 months and generating regular income for 3 to 4 years before needing replacement. For a farmer who needs cash flow while longer-term crops establish, it is hard to beat.

The Virus Challenge

Papaya Ring Spot Virus is the most serious constraint on papaya production worldwide. It spreads through aphids and can devastate an entire planting within months of establishment. The organic management strategy is built around three principles: start clean, suppress vectors, and remove infected plants immediately before the virus spreads further.

Starting with certified virus-free tissue culture seedlings is the most important investment you can make. Reflective silver mulch on the soil surface confuses aphids — they cannot find the plant to land on, dramatically reducing virus transmission. Neem oil sprayed every 10 days from establishment helps suppress aphid populations further.

Organic Soil and Nutrition

Papaya is a gross feeder with shallow roots. It responds dramatically to soil organic matter and will almost certainly disappoint on poor soils regardless of how much fertiliser is applied. Before planting, dig a pit 60cm cubed and fill with a 50/50 mix of topsoil and mature compost. Apply 5kg of compost per plant every 6 weeks during the growing season, and mulch the root zone heavily to maintain soil moisture and temperature.

⚠️ Never Let Papaya Sit in Water

Papaya root systems are highly susceptible to Phytophthora collar rot in waterlogged conditions. Even 24 hours of standing water can fatally damage a plant. Raised beds or mounded planting positions are strongly recommended in areas with heavy rainfall or poorly draining soils.

Jackfruit: The Low-Maintenance Giant With Enormous Potential

Jackfruit asks for almost nothing and gives back enormously. Here is how to grow it right.

📖 9 min read🌿 GreenHarvest

Among all tropical fruit trees, jackfruit may be the most forgiving. It tolerates poor soils, periodic drought, high humidity, and benign neglect with a generosity that no other large-fruiting tree can match. A well-sited jackfruit tree demands very little from a farmer while producing fruit that can weigh 50 kilograms — and doing so for decades.

The global market for jackfruit has grown remarkably over the past decade, driven largely by its adoption as a meat substitute in vegetarian and vegan cooking. Young, unripe jackfruit has a fibrous texture that mimics pulled pork or chicken with remarkable fidelity. This has opened markets in Europe and North America that simply did not exist ten years ago.

Growing Organically — The Easy Case

Jackfruit's natural resilience means that organic management is genuinely straightforward. The tree evolved in conditions where no one was applying fertilisers or pesticides, and its default growth habit reflects that. Annual compost applications of 15–20kg per mature tree and a consistent mulch layer are the primary inputs needed for good production.

The most valuable organic input for jackfruit is green manure cropped from the spaces between young trees. Legumes like Gliricidia or Sesbania, chopped and incorporated into the soil annually, can supply all the nitrogen a developing jackfruit orchard needs for the first 5 years while simultaneously building the organic matter content of the soil.

🌍 Market Timing

The global young jackfruit market — primarily for unripe fruit sold as a meat substitute — commands significantly higher prices than ripe fruit in many regions. Understanding this distinction before harvest can dramatically affect the economics of a jackfruit enterprise. Young fruit harvested at 10–12 weeks after fruit set is the product that western food manufacturers want.

Guava: The Toughest Fruit Tree You'll Ever Love

Guava thrives where other fruit trees give up. Here is how to manage it for maximum yield without a single chemical input.

📖 8 min read🌿 GreenHarvest

Guava has a reputation for being difficult to manage, and it earns that reputation for one specific reason: without regular pruning, a guava orchard becomes an impenetrable thicket within a few seasons. But manage its vigorous growth well, and guava will reward you with two to three crops per year, reliable yields even in drought conditions, and very little pest pressure compared to most fruit trees.

The Pruning-First Philosophy

Everything in guava management revolves around pruning. Guavas fruit on new growth, which means the more you encourage new shoots, the more you fruit. After each harvest cycle, cut back the main fruiting branches by one-third. This sounds aggressive but the tree responds with vigorous new growth that becomes your next fruiting wood within 8–10 weeks.

Maintain an open canopy shape — light penetration to the inner branches is critical for fruit quality and for managing the humid microclimate that encourages fungal disease. In humid regions especially, dense canopies create ideal conditions for guava wilt, which is the most serious disease this crop faces.

Organic Nutrition

Guava is a moderate feeder that responds well to compost and declines on compacted, waterlogged soils. Apply 10–15kg of compost per tree twice yearly and maintain a mulch layer that extends to the canopy drip line. The guava's root system is shallow and wide — nutrients and moisture placed at the surface are more accessible to the tree than deep applications.

🏆 Guava's Hidden Advantage

Guava Vitamin C content is among the highest of any common fruit — 4 to 5 times higher than orange. In markets where nutritional claims drive purchasing decisions, this is a genuine selling point that organic guava farmers can use to distinguish their fruit from conventionally grown alternatives.

Pomegranate Farming: Ancient Fruit, Modern Organic Approach

Pomegranate has been farmed for 5,000 years. The organic methods that work best today are not far removed from what worked then.

📖 9 min read🌿 GreenHarvest

Pomegranate is a drought-tolerant, long-lived crop that was being cultivated organically by default long before the term existed. It evolved in the semi-arid regions of Persia and the Himalayas — conditions that select for resilience over dependence on inputs. Modern organic management of pomegranate simply formalises what traditional farmers in those regions have always known.

Why Pomegranate Suits Organic Systems

Pomegranate's deep root system, drought tolerance, and generally low pest pressure make it a natural fit for organic management. The crop's most serious challenges — fruit cracking, bacterial blight, and fruit boring insects — are all manageable through cultural and biological methods without synthetic chemistry.

Fruit cracking, which is the most common quality problem, is almost entirely a water management issue. Irregular irrigation — long dry periods followed by sudden heavy watering — causes rapid fruit expansion that the skin cannot accommodate. A consistent, moderate irrigation regime through the fruit-filling period prevents cracking far more effectively than any spray programme.

Soil and Organic Inputs

Pomegranate tolerates poor, rocky, alkaline soils better than almost any other fruit crop. But tolerance is not the same as preference — on deep, well-amended soils with consistent compost applications, the same variety will produce fruit twice the size and with significantly better colour and flavour than on marginal land.

Annual applications of 10–15kg compost per tree, combined with a green manure cover crop between rows that is chopped and incorporated before flowering, provides all the macronutrients a well-established pomegranate orchard needs. Supplementing with 500g of bone meal per tree per year addresses phosphorus needs without chemical fertilisers.

🌺 Flower Drop vs Fruit Set

Pomegranate produces far more flowers than it can set as fruit. Bisexual flowers with a swollen base below the petals will set fruit. Male-only flowers with a narrow base will drop. This is normal — do not mistake normal flower drop for a pollination failure. A good pomegranate crop requires only 5–10% of flowers to set fruit.

Fig Tree Cultivation: Drought-Tolerant, Chemical-Free and Rewarding

The fig is one of the oldest cultivated fruits and one of the easiest to grow organically. Here is everything you need to know.

📖 8 min read🌿 GreenHarvest

The fig tree has been cultivated for longer than any other fruit tree. Archaeobotanical evidence suggests cultivation began at least 11,000 years ago — predating wheat and barley. That extraordinary history is not coincidental. The fig is simply one of the most reliable, productive, and low-maintenance food plants that humans have ever domesticated.

Growing figs organically is not a compromise — it is the natural state of the crop. Figs have low synthetic fertiliser requirements, minimal serious pest pressure in most climates, and produce abundantly on soils where more demanding crops would fail.

Understanding the Fig's Unusual Biology

What we call a fig "fruit" is technically a syconium — an inverted flower structure where hundreds of tiny flowers develop inside the fleshy receptacle we eat. In some traditional fig varieties, pollination requires a symbiotic relationship with a specific fig wasp species. Modern commercial varieties are typically parthenocarpic — they produce fruit without pollination — which eliminates this complexity entirely.

Organic Growing Basics

Figs are among the most drought-tolerant of all fruit trees. Their deep root systems can access moisture far below the surface in established trees, making them suitable for dryland organic farming systems where irrigation is limited or unavailable. Young trees need supplemental water for the first 2–3 years, but once established they can survive on rainfall alone in most climates where they are adapted.

For organic nutrition, figs respond primarily to potassium. Wood ash applied at 1–2kg per tree twice yearly is the simplest and most cost-effective potassium source for organic growers. Combined with annual compost applications, this is sufficient to maintain productivity in most situations.

🌞 The Drying Opportunity

Dried figs command 3–5 times the price of fresh figs in most markets and have a shelf life of 12+ months compared to fresh figs' 2–3 days. If you have consistent hot, dry weather during harvest season, sun-drying figs on mesh racks is a zero-cost value addition that transforms the economics of a fig enterprise entirely.
